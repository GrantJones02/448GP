Setting up the Board

Throughout the lesson on the types of pieces,
a board set up in the starting position was used.

To provide a formal instruction, however,
here are the basic steps to set up the board.

Setting up the Board

1. Each player has their own edge,
where the leftmost square should be dark
and the rightmost square should be light.

2. The pawns go on the row in front of the edge.

3. On each player's back row,
in order from the corners to the center is
rook, knight, bishop, and then king/queen.

4. Your queen starts on her own color.

Opening The Game

As you saw in the exercise on the previous page,
not all of your pieces are able to be moved
on the first turn.

Pieces that cannot be moved in the current position
are often called "inactive".

Your active pieces at the beginning
can be used to start the game with
what is called an opening.

Opening The Game

The most common pieces to open with
for both colors are
the E Pawn (King's Pawn),
the D Pawn (Queen's Pawn),
and the Knights.

This is because these pieces
allow you to activate
other pieces on the board,
and you can also use these pieces
to control the center.

Simple Strategy

Mentioned in the previous section,
one of the most important tactics
is to control the center of the board.

This allows you to make the most attacks
against your opponent at once
while also defending your king from danger.

Your goal from here will be to land
a check, or possibly even checkmate
against your opponent from an offensive position.

Check

Check is one of three special states
that the board can be placed into by the players.

Your king is in check if
your opponent could capture your king
on their next turn.

Check

When you are placed into check,
you have to make a move that
takes you out of check.

Eliminating the threat to your king
is not optional.

The three common ways to do this are:
1. Capturing the threatening piece
2. Shielding with a different piece
3. Moving your king

Checkmate

When you are placed into check,
but there is no move you can make
that will remove you from check,
you are in the second special board state:
CHECKMATE.

If you checkmate your opponent,
you have won the game.

Most checkmates involve trapping the king
using the combined power of multiple pieces.

Stalemate and Draws

The third special board state
is STALEMATE.

Stalemate occurs when the player
whose turn it is to move
has no legal moves they can make.
(Which happens more often that you might think!)

This is also an end to the game,
but neither player wins in this scenario.

If you are winning, you want to avoid this outcome.
If you are at risk of losing, on the other hand,
sometimes your best outcome is try to
force a stalemate instead.

Stalemate and Draws

Stalemate is almost always caused
by limiting the movement of the king.

The king can never move into check,
so if no other piece can move
you can accidentally force a stalemate
by boxing the king in without attacking him directly.

The best way to avoid accidentally causing stalemate
when you have an advantage over your opponent
is to place them in check each turn.

You can never be in check and stalemate
at the same time.

Stalemate and Draws

There are other ways to draw the game
that are present in tournament styles of Chess.

These are added to prevent certain strategies
that involve waiting on your opponent to slip up
rather than employing better strategy.

Three-fold repetition is a rule added
that forces a draw if the same position happens
three times in a row.

Similarly, the fifty-turn rule has been added
to force a draw if players go fifty turns without
changing the balance of power,
either by one player capturing another's pieces
or one player making a pawn move.

Optional Rules

In chess tournaments,
you will sometimes see a chess clock used.

These start with a set time,
that runs down only when you are on your turn.

You start your clock when your turn begins,
and only get to stop it when you make your move.

These aren't often used in casual play,
but if you want to learn how to play competitively
it does not hurt to practice with one of these.
