Lesson 6: Defending

Lesson Introduction

Context

In the previous lesson, terminology
like threatening and defending
was used to describe the interactions
between certain pieces on the board.

This lesson will explain the framework
to think about how pieces interact this way.

Material

A crucial concept to understand
to understand chess strategy
is material or point value, a number that represents
the approximate value of a piece.

Conventionally:
- A pawn is worth one point
- A knight is worth three points
- A bishop is worth three points
- A rook is worth five points
- A queen is worth nine points

And your king is priceless, of course.

Trades

The point system is not gospel,
nor is it a series of hard and fast rules.

Rather, quantifying the value of pieces
allows us to talk about chess like
an economist or a strategist:
in terms of opportunity costs
and trade-offs.

Trades

In chess, a trade
is an interaction between two pieces
where you give up something of some point value
in exchange for something of another point value.

Every interaction in chess
is a trade of some form,
good trade or bad trade,
simple trade or complex trade.

Trades

Your goal is to maximize the value
gained from an interaction,
to tip the scales
as far in your favor as possible.

Your opponent is thinking about this, too,
and it is useful to consider whether your opponent
would or would not act on something
as part of evaluating the value of an interaction.

Threatening

One piece threatens another
if it would be reasonable to state
that the piece would capture the other
if the player was given a turn to do so.

In this position, on the other hand,

Defending

A piece, in contrast, is defended
if a piece that captured it
would be under threat
of capture itself.

In the previous position,
White's rook is defending
White's pawn.

Defending

You should make moves broadly
that are defended by previous moves.

Having a chain of pieces defended
by the previous one back in the chain
allows you to push deep into enemy territory
while your opponent has to pay a steep price
to break up your offensive.

A good offense always has
a good defense backing it up.

Forks

The final topic for this lesson
is forks.
A fork is a situation where
two pieces are being threatened
by the same source.

Here, you can predict that the player
whose pieces are being threatened
will choose to save the piece
with the higher material value.

Forks

In this example, the black bishop
threatens both the white rook and knight.
However, Black would choose the rook over the knight.

Forks

This fork then forces White
to sacrifice the value of a knight
over the next turn to save their rook.

Forks

Forks are a solid offensive measure
that can break up the defenses of an opponent
by forcing them to choose who to save.
